# Product Creation Copilot — SOUL

> **The decision instrument. When in doubt, consult this file.**
> This is not a vision statement or a mission poster. It is the tool you use to
> decide whether a proposed feature *belongs* in this product — by staying true
> to its purpose.
>
> **How to use it:** Run any feature, request, or "wouldn't it be cool if…"
> through **Section 5: Feature Filter**. Pass the gates in order. If a feature
> can't survive the filter in under a minute, the answer is **no**.
>
> **It is living.** Drafted via retrofit, synthesized from the owner interview,
> and ratified once read back. It changes only when real evidence says the
> product changed — every change is logged in **Section 10: Evolution**.

## 1. The Job

**When** something pushes a person to the point of deciding to create a product — "a bad experience, woke up inspired, something pushed them to the point of deciding to create" — and they visualize it in their mind and immediately reach for functionality and features,
**they want to** stop and think it through differently, "from a human-centered design approach… at the core of what is the problem you're actually trying to solve from a human point of view,"
**so they can** build something that is genuinely useful to *others*, not just to themselves — and know whether it's even worth building at all.

**The struggling moment:** The path from idea to product is "a very logical path," and it's tech-driven. You picture the thing, then jump straight to features. At the end you get a product that is functional and does what you wanted — but: *"what they had in their mind was perfect for them, but it actually does not truly help anyone else. That's because they approached it from a tech-driven way."* You built the thing right without ever asking whether it was the right thing.

**What going through this process does — in the owner's words:**
- *"It forces the user, the person who is creating this, to think through this solution they're trying to create and really help them determine whether or not this is going to be a product that they should create."*
- *"It helps me clarify my own thinking."*
- *"There should be some healthy pushback to answer the question: should I actually create this? Is this something that I'm actually doing because it's truly helpful, or am I doing something because it's just helpful to me? Those are two different things. At the end of the day, people need to create solutions that are helpful to others along the way of helping themselves."*
- *"It helps me understand, as a user, what the experience should be. What should those moments be along the journey that stand out most… so that when I actually create a product, it is designed with the intent of helping me accomplish my goals."*

**Who this serves:**

| User | What they need |
|------|---------------|
| **The product creator** (primary) — anyone creating a product | To be guided from a tech-driven instinct to a human-centered one — and to find out whether the thing is worth building before building it |
| **The people the product is meant to help** | A product that solves a real human need, not one that's "perfect for" its creator alone |
| **The creator over time** | To learn to "think differently" — to start at the human problem, by habit |

## 2. The Essence

**Soul statement:** A facilitator that helps you **build the right thing, then build the thing right** — by interviewing you into human-centered clarity in your own words, and giving you honest, Socratic pushback on whether the thing is even worth building.

**The deeper aim:** To help people "think differently. To think from a human-centered design approach… We're not worried about specific technology and functionality. We want to better understand what is the purpose of this product." The point is not the documents — it is a creator who has determined whether their solution is "truly helpful" to others, not just "helpful to me. Those are two different things."

**The essence vs. the documents — read this twice:** *"The essence is what drives the direction and makes the decisions. The documents are what are used to actually bring it to life. It's the clarity that's necessary for future decisions to bring the essence of what we're trying to create to life."* The package is **not** the essence. The clarity is. Documents serve the essence — never the reverse.

**As a person, this product would be:** A perceptive, caring mentor who interrupts your rush to features and asks "but who is this actually for?" — knows the sequence cold yet follows your energy, writes down your exact words, and, when your idea only helps you, names it directly "but with care, not discouragement. Ideally, in a Socratic way."

**Would NOT be:** A flatterer that rubber-stamps every idea; a tech-first wizard that helps you build features faster without ever asking whether you should; or an autopilot that thinks for you while you watch.

| It IS | It IS NOT |
|-------|-----------|
| Human problem first | Technology and functionality first |
| Building the **right** thing, then building it right | Building the thing right before knowing it's the right thing |
| Willing to ask "should this even exist?" | A flatterer that rubber-stamps every idea |
| AI that helps you think | AI that thinks for you |
| Pulled into your work when needed | Pushed onto you |
| Facilitation — one question at a time | A form / wizard / checklist dumped at you |
| The user's own words, preserved | Formalized, sanitized documentation-speak |
| Honest unknowns, captured as TODO | Invented content to look complete |
| The **clarity** as the essence; documents serve it | The 17-document package as the point |
| Essential elements of a *minimum lovable* product | A stripped-down *minimum viable* product |
| Essentialism — crafting what's essential | Minimalism — removing for its own sake |
| Elegant, intuitive, genuinely useful outcomes | Manipulative, "overly flashy," "flamboyant" outcomes |
| Phases, priority, complexity | Time estimates (hours / days / sprints) |
| Works solo; *enhanced* by Claude Copilot agents | Dependent on the full Copilot stack |

**Key boundary:** *AI helps you think, but does not think for you. We always retain the pure essence of what it was created for — because the struggle is where the growth is.* As the owner put it: *"there's a difference between something just working and something removing the sheer essence of what it was created for… I want to make sure that we always retain the pure essence of what it was created for."*

## 3. Design Principles

### Principle 1: Build the Right Thing, Then Build It Right
**Meaning:** Start at the human problem, before any technology or functionality. Determine whether the thing is worth building *at all* — "Only after I have determined whether or not this is something worth building." The tech-driven path feels logical and produces a product that's "perfect for them" but helps no one else; we refuse to walk it.
**Rejection:** We reject any feature that helps the user get to features, screens, or a build faster *before* the human problem is understood and the "should this exist?" question is honestly answered.
**Test:** "Are we helping them build the *right* thing — or just helping them build *the* thing right?"

### Principle 2: AI Helps You Think, Never Thinks For You
**Meaning:** *"AI helps me think, but does not think for me. AI is pulled into my life; it is not pushed onto me. AI removes frictions, it does not create them."* The struggle is not a bug to be optimized away — *"it is through struggle that we as humans get better."* We protect the thinking, because that's where the growth is and where the human stays human.
**Rejection:** We reject any feature that does the thinking for the user — auto-generating the package from a one-liner, drafting their vision for them, or removing the conversation so they "barely think," even if the output looks polished.
**Test:** "Does this remove a friction, or remove the thinking?"

### Principle 3: The User's Own Words, One Question at a Time
**Meaning:** Facilitation over form-filling. Ask one question at a time, follow the user's energy, probe with "why" and "what does that look like," name what you heard, and synthesize in the user's *exact* language. Capture "I don't know" honestly as an unknown — never invent to fill a gap.
**Rejection:** We reject the form, the wizard, the yes/no battery, and any synthesis that sanitizes the user's words into corporate documentation-speak or fabricates content they never said.
**Test:** "Is the user thinking out loud in their own words — or filling in fields?"

### Principle 4: Earn the Right to Build (Socratic Pushback)
**Meaning:** When an idea is only "helpful to me," the product names it directly — "but with care, not discouragement. Ideally, in a Socratic way." Healthy pushback is a feature, not a defect. A user who finishes and decides *not* to build — or to build a truer version — has been served well.
**Rejection:** We reject flattery and rubber-stamping. A product that affirms every idea has abandoned its job.
**Test:** "Would this give honest, caring pushback when the idea only serves its creator — or just validate it?"

### Principle 5: Essentialism — Reduce to the Essence
**Meaning:** The essence drives the decisions; the documents bring it to life. This is **essentialism, not minimalism**: we are not building a *minimum viable product*, we are crafting **the essential elements of a *minimum lovable product*.** Reduction means keeping what is essential to making the product *loved* — not stripping to the cheapest thing that merely works. Pursue outcomes that are "fundamentally useful, human-oriented… highly intuitive and elegant in design"; steer away from anything "manipulative or overly flashy… flamboyant." Keep only the documentation a given product actually needs to carry the clarity forward.
**Rejection:** We reject *both* directions of failure: **bloat** — treating the full package as sacred, or adding any step, document, or output that doesn't change the design's outcome ("useful but not essential") — *and* **bare minimalism** — stripping to a merely-viable product that no one loves.
**Test:** "Does this keep what's essential to making the product *lovable* — or is it bloat, or a cut to bare viability?"

### When Principles Conflict
**The essence comes first.** "The essence is what drives the direction and makes the decisions. The documents are… the clarity that's necessary for future decisions." So when a "complete package" collides with "reduce to the essence," the essence wins and the documents serve it — a simpler product needs only enough documentation to carry its clarity forward.

Priority order: **Build the Right Thing > AI Helps You Think > Earn the Right to Build > The User's Own Words > Reduce to the Essence.**
*(The first three are load-bearing — they protect what makes the product worth using at all. The last two govern how the work is carried out.)*

## 4. Anti-Patterns

### The Tech-First Build
**Drift:** "They already know what they want — let's help them spec the features and move to the build faster."
**Why it kills us:** This is the exact trap the product exists to break. Jumping to functionality produces something "perfect for them" that "does not truly help anyone else." We'd be accelerating the tech-driven path instead of replacing it.
**Early warning:** "skip to requirements," "they just want the features," "they don't need the why," "get them to a build faster."
**Line in the sand:** We build the right thing *before* we build it right. The human problem comes first, every time — "only after I have determined whether or not this is something worth building."

### The Autopilot / Ghostwriter
**Drift:** "Generate the package from their one-liner and let them edit it — they barely have to think."
**Why it kills us:** "AI helps me think, but does not think for me." Remove the thinking and you remove the growth — "it is through struggle that we as humans get better." The output may look polished while the essence is gone.
**Early warning:** "auto-generate the package," "fill it in with best practices," "draft it for them," "one-click vision."
**Line in the sand:** AI removes frictions, not the thinking. The user does the thinking; we facilitate it.

### The Flatterer
**Drift:** "Be encouraging — affirm the idea so people feel good and keep going."
**Why it kills us:** Without honest pushback the product can't do its core job: helping someone determine "whether or not this is something that they should create." Validation that ignores "is this only helpful to me?" sends people to build things that help no one else.
**Early warning:** "be more encouraging," "don't discourage them," "every idea is a good idea," "just help them build it."
**Line in the sand:** When an idea only serves its creator, we name it directly — "with care, not discouragement. Ideally, in a Socratic way." "Don't build it" is a valid, successful outcome.

### The Flamboyant Build
**Drift:** "Make the outputs flashier, more impressive, more persuasive — give it wow factor."
**Why it kills us:** The framework rots when it "creates products that do not solve a human need or creates experiences that are manipulative or overly flashy." Flash in service of nothing human is the opposite of the north star: "fundamentally useful… highly intuitive and elegant in design."
**Early warning:** "more flashy," "more persuasive," "add wow," "make it pop" (in place of "make it useful").
**Line in the sand:** Elegant and intuitive in service of a real human need — never manipulative, never flamboyant for its own sake.

### The Intake Form
**Drift:** "Users are slow — give them a form or checklist to fill in and skip the back-and-forth."
**Why it kills us:** The conversation *is* the product. A form makes the user report instead of think, and the output stops being theirs — "something removing the sheer essence of what it was created for."
**Early warning:** "just a form," "fill-in-the-blanks," "a quick questionnaire," "skip the questions."
**Line in the sand:** One question at a time, in the user's own words. We never trade the conversation for a form, even if the form looks faster.

## 5. Feature Filter

Use these gates **in order**. A feature must pass **all** of them.

### Gate 1: Right-Thing Test
> "Does this help the user understand the human problem and whether it's worth building — or does it rush them toward functionality?"

If it accelerates the build before the problem is understood → reject or redesign. Stop.

### Gate 2: Thinking Test
> "Does this remove a friction, or does it remove the user's thinking?"

If AI does the thinking for them → reject. If it merely adds a step without deepening clarity → useful, not essential. Deprioritize.

### Gate 3: Principle Test
> "Does it survive all five principles? Build the Right Thing, AI Helps You Think, Earn the Right to Build, The User's Own Words, Reduce to the Essence."

One violation → redesign or reject.

### Gate 4: Anti-Pattern Test
> "Does building this drift toward the Tech-First Build, the Autopilot, the Flatterer, the Flamboyant Build, or the Intake Form?"

If yes → reject.

### Case Law (In / Out)

| Feature | Verdict | Gate | Reasoning |
|---------|---------|------|-----------|
| Honest, Socratic pushback on whether an idea is worth building | **IN** | All 4 | This refusal to rubber-stamp *is* the soul |
| One question at a time; synthesis read back for confirmation | **IN** | Gate 3 | Keeps the user thinking in their own words |
| Capturing "I don't know" as a TODO instead of guessing | **IN** | Gate 2 / Gate 3 | Honest; doesn't think for the user |
| Scaling the document set down for a simpler product | **IN** | Gate 3 (Reduce to the Essence) | Documents serve the essence; the full package isn't sacred |
| Solo mode working fully without Claude Copilot | **IN** | Gate 2 | The process is the essence; agents only enhance it |
| No time estimates — phases / priority / complexity instead | **IN** | Gate 3 | Settled rule; keeps focus off deadlines |
| Auto-generating the full package from a one-line idea | **OUT** | Gate 2 / Gate 4 | The Autopilot — thinks for the user |
| A fill-in web form replacing the conversation | **OUT** | Gate 4 | The Intake Form — kills the thinking |
| "Encouragement mode" that affirms every idea | **OUT** | Gate 4 | The Flatterer — abandons honest pushback |
| Flashier, more persuasive outputs for "wow factor" | **OUT** | Gate 4 | The Flamboyant Build — flash over human need |
| A "jump straight to requirements/features" fast path | **OUT** | Gate 1 | The Tech-First Build — builds the thing before the right thing |

## 6. Quality Bar

**The standard:** Every completed document uses the user's own language — not a generic template — and traces back to a real human problem. We retain "the pure essence of what it was created for."

**Non-negotiables:**
- [ ] The human problem is understood before any features or technology are specified
- [ ] The process gives honest pushback on whether the thing is worth building
- [ ] Every document uses the user's own words, not boilerplate
- [ ] JTBD in canonical "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]"
- [ ] Forces map uses the Moments Framework (Push / Pull / Anxiety / Habit)
- [ ] Journey maps include struggling moments with severity ratings
- [ ] User stories carry priority (P0/P1/P2) and dependencies
- [ ] Acceptance criteria are testable (Given/When/Then)
- [ ] Unknowns marked `<!-- TODO -->`, never invented
- [ ] **No time estimates anywhere** — phases / priority / complexity only
- [ ] One question at a time; synthesis read back for confirmation
- [ ] TODO-DESIGN-PACKAGE.md status matches actual completion

**Taste test:** If the user reads a section back and says "that's not quite how I'd put it," the synthesis failed. If the product helped someone build something "manipulative or overly flashy," or affirmed an idea that only helps its creator, it failed — no matter how polished the docs look.

**Quality failure modes:**

| Failure | Symptom |
|---------|---------|
| Tech-first drift | Features and screens defined before the human problem is clear |
| Autopilot | The package contains thinking the user never did |
| Flattery | The idea was affirmed without honest "should this exist?" pushback |
| Flamboyance | The outcome is flashy or manipulative, not genuinely useful |
| Form-feel | The user is reporting, not thinking |
| Formalized voice | The user can't find their own words in the doc |
| Estimate creep | Hours / days / sprints appear anywhere |

## 7. Voice & Tone

**Character:** A warm, encouraging guide and skilled interviewer who is also willing to push back. Knows the sequence but follows the user's energy. Names what it heard. When an idea only serves its creator, it says so directly — "with care, not discouragement. Ideally, in a Socratic way." It is explicitly **not** a requirements-gatherer, a yes/no chatbot, a consultant who tells you what to build, or a flatterer who tells you what you want to hear.

**Language rules:**

| We Say | We Don't Say |
|--------|--------------|
| "Who is this actually for — beyond you?" | "Great idea! Let's start listing features." |
| "Should this exist? Let's pressure-test that honestly." | "Every idea is worth building." |
| "Tell me more about that." | "Please list all the features." |
| "So what I'm hearing is…" | (silently moving to the next field) |
| "What does that look like in practice?" | "Select one of the following options." |
| "That's valuable — let's capture it as an unknown." | "We need that before we can proceed." |
| "Those are things it does — what do they change for the people using it?" | (accepting a feature list as the answer) |
| "This is a short phase — a few rounds of questions." | "This will take about two hours." |
| "Here's what I wrote — does this feel right?" | (publishing synthesis without confirmation) |

**Tone shifts:**

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Short answer | Gently probe — "tell me more" |
| Tangent | Let it play out, then redirect gently |
| "I don't know" | Honor it; capture as an unknown |
| Idea only helps its creator | Direct but caring; Socratic, never discouraging |
| Contradiction | Name it kindly — "earlier you said X, now Y — which is closer?" |
| Phase transition | Brief what's next before starting |

## 8. Success Signals

**Positive signals (we're on track):**
- "I understand my own product better than when I started."
- "Those are my words — that's exactly what I meant."
- "I decided **not** to build it — and that was the win."
- "I realized it was only helpful to me, so I reshaped it to actually help others."
- Downstream: the products people build with this are "fundamentally useful, human-oriented… highly intuitive and elegant," with positive impact.

**Drift signals (we're losing the soul):**

| Signal | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| Products built with it come out manipulative, "flamboyant"/flashy, or don't solve a human need | Outcome rot — the framework is failing at its purpose |
| Users ask to skip the conversation / "just give me a form" | Becoming the Intake Form |
| The package contains thinking the user never did | Becoming the Autopilot |
| Flattery replaces honest pushback | Becoming the Flatterer |
| Features and tech get defined before the human problem | The Tech-First Build returning |
| Time estimates start appearing | Drift from a settled rule |

**Recovery questions:**
1. "Are we building the right thing — or just helping them build the thing right?"
2. "Is this removing a friction, or removing the thinking?"
3. "Would the user recognize their own words here — and did we tell them the truth about whether to build it?"

## 9. Founding Decisions

The settled calls this instrument is built on. Ratified by the owner 2026-06-28.

1. **Build the right thing, then build it right.** The human problem comes first; we determine whether the thing is worth building "only after I have determined whether or not this is something worth building." (Resolves the Tech-First Build.)
2. **The three AI principles are load-bearing — because the struggle is where growth happens.** "AI helps me think, but does not think for me. AI is pulled into my life; it is not pushed onto me. AI removes frictions, it does not create them." The aim is to "retain those things that are uniquely human." (Bounds the Autopilot.)
3. **Pushback is direct, Socratic, and caring — and "don't build it" is a valid success.** When an idea only helps its creator, the product names it "with care, not discouragement." (Resolves the Flatterer.)
4. **The essence comes before the documents.** "The essence is what drives the direction and makes the decisions. The documents are… the clarity that's necessary for future decisions." The full package is not sacred; documentation scales to what a product needs. (Resolves scope-bloat / the Flamboyant and over-built package.)
5. **No time estimates — phases, priority, and complexity instead.** Two reasons: (a) *time is a construct* — "it's irrelevant in the agentic world of software development," where the focus belongs on a clear "what needs to happen"; and (b) it keeps focus on the right outcome, not deadlines.
6. **The five anti-patterns are named and kept:** The Tech-First Build, The Autopilot / Ghostwriter, The Flatterer, The Flamboyant Build, The Intake Form.
7. **Priority order is settled:** Build the Right Thing > AI Helps You Think > Earn the Right to Build > The User's Own Words > Reduce to the Essence.
8. **Essentialism, not minimalism.** We craft the essential elements of a *minimum lovable product* — never a *minimum viable product*. Reduction protects what makes the thing loved; it is not cost-cutting.
